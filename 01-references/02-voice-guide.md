**Christian Copy Voice: A Calibration Guide**

**The Four Pillars of the Voice**

The voice that converts on Christian sales pages rests on four pillars. Each one is non-negotiable, and they reinforce each other. Drop any single pillar and the voice collapses into either generic marketing copy or churchy in-group writing - both of which underperform.

**Warm.** The reader is treated as a person the writer cares about, not a transaction to be closed. Warmth shows up in word choice, sentence rhythm, and the willingness to slow down at emotionally charged moments rather than rush past them. A warm sentence acknowledges the reader's situation before asking anything of them.

**Urgent.** Warmth without urgency is sentimentality, and Christian audiences respect urgency that is honestly earned. The reader is in a real situation - pain, confusion, drift, longing - and time matters. The voice never apologizes for naming this. Urgency in this skill is never manufactured (see philosophy.md); it is the natural urgency of a friend who genuinely believes the reader needs what is on offer and would feel guilty not saying so plainly.

**Plain.** The vocabulary stays at roughly an eighth-grade reading level throughout, regardless of how theologically mature the audience is. This is not because Christian readers cannot handle complex language - many can - but because plain language is what crosses denominational lines, reaches the hurting reader whose mental bandwidth is reduced by their pain, and respects the reader's time. The most spiritually mature writers consistently use the plainest words.

**Second-person.** The page speaks to _you_, the reader, throughout. First-person should appear only when telling the author's testimony or origin story. Third-person ("many believers struggle with...") is the death of conversion copy and should be cut wherever it appears. The reader's experience is the subject of every paragraph, even the paragraphs about the offer.

**The Rhythm of Christian Copy**

Christian copy has a specific cadence that distinguishes it from secular direct-response writing. Get the rhythm wrong and the copy reads as either generic marketing in religious dress, or as a sermon trying to sell something. Both fail.

The rhythm alternates between two registers. The first is conversational, warm, present-tense, second-person. It speaks directly to the reader's current moment. The second is reflective, scriptural, occasionally first-person plural ("we"), grounded in truth larger than the moment. It zooms out to eternal perspective.

A page that stays in the first register reads as shallow. A page that stays in the second register reads as preachy. The conversion happens in the movement between them - speaking to the reader's real life, then anchoring that life in scripture, then returning to the reader's real life with the scripture now applied.

A simple pattern:

_You know that feeling - the one that hits at 3 a.m., when the house is quiet and the weight of it all settles on your chest. You've prayed. You've waited. You've read the verses you were taught to read. And still, the morning comes and the weight is still there._

_Paul knew this weight. Not metaphorically. Literally. Beaten, imprisoned, abandoned, betrayed - and writing, from a Roman cell, that he had learned the secret of being content in any circumstance. Not because the circumstance changed. Because something inside him did._

_That "something" is what you're after. And it isn't a feeling. It's a framework. And it's the framework these ten books were written to walk you into._

Notice the movement: reader's moment, scripture and biblical figure, return to reader with the offer named. This three-beat rhythm - _moment, anchor, return_ - is the engine of the voice.

**When to Use Brother / Sister / Friend Language**

The terms _brother_ and _sister_ are powerful and overused. They signal in-group warmth when deployed sparingly, and they signal performative Christian marketing when deployed constantly.

Use them rarely. A long sales page might use _brother_ or _sister_ one or two times total, placed at moments of high emotional weight - typically near the close, after the offer has been made and the reader is deciding. "Brother, if you've read this far, you already know" hits differently than "Sister, are you tired of feeling alone in your suffering?" The first treats the term as a moment of recognition. The second uses it as a sales hook, and Christian readers feel the difference.

_Friend_ is safer and more flexible. It crosses denominational lines, works for both genders, and carries warmth without claiming a specific spiritual relationship the writer has not established with the reader. _Friend_ can appear two or three times in a page without losing weight.

Never open a sales page with _brother_, _sister_, or _friend_. The reader has not earned the right to be called that yet, and the writer has not earned the right to call them that. These terms belong in the middle and late sections of the page, after enough has been said that the reader feels seen.

Avoid entirely: _beloved_, _saint_, _child of God_, _my dear one_, _precious one_. These read as performative or condescending in marketing contexts, regardless of how genuinely the writer means them.

**Denominational Calibration**

The default posture of this skill is broadly evangelical - language and framing that works across Baptist, non-denominational, charismatic, Reformed, Methodist, Pentecostal, and most Bible-church audiences without alienating any of them. This is the widest commercially viable audience for most Christian digital products.

Within that default, several specific landmines exist.

**The charismatic / cessationist divide.** Language about prophetic words, words of knowledge, present-tense apostolic gifts, healing on demand, and direct supernatural encounters will energize charismatic readers and instantly lose Reformed and cessationist readers. Conversely, language that explicitly denies or minimizes the present work of the Holy Spirit will lose charismatic readers. The neutral path uses biblical language that both camps share: _the Holy Spirit comforts, convicts, guides, and strengthens_. Specific gift claims should appear only when the author's audience is clearly within one camp.

**The Calvinist / Arminian divide.** Sovereignty language - _God ordained your suffering_, _every detail of your pain was written before time_ - energizes Reformed readers and unsettles Arminian and free-will-emphasizing readers. Free-will language - _God is responding to your choices_, _your faith activates God's hand_ - does the reverse. The neutral path emphasizes God's character (faithful, present, sovereign over outcomes) without making specific philosophical claims about how His sovereignty relates to human choice. Phrases like _God meets you in this_ work across both camps.

**Prosperity vs. suffering-affirming theology.** The two are often opposed, but a sales page on suffering must be careful not to swing so hard against prosperity teaching that it reads as theologically combative. Most readers do not want to be drawn into doctrinal disputes; they want help with their pain. State the suffering-affirming view positively ("God is shaping you through this") rather than negatively ("this isn't punishment, despite what some teachers claim").

**Catholic and Orthodox readers.** Most evangelically-pitched copy will exclude these readers by default through Protestant-coded language. If the audience includes Catholic or Orthodox readers, avoid Reformation-flavored phrases like _Bible alone_, _faith alone_, and _priesthood of all believers_. Use _the church_, _the saints who walked before you_, and _the long tradition of Christian suffering_ - language that resonates ecumenically.

When in doubt about denominational range, ask the author during intake (see templates/intake-questionnaire.md) and calibrate accordingly. If the answer is "as broad as possible," default to evangelical-neutral and avoid all four landmines above.

**Words and Phrases to Avoid**

Several words and phrases reliably lower conversion on Christian sales pages, either because they trigger manipulation alarms, sound clichéd, or read as performative.

**Manipulation triggers:**

- _Secret_, _hidden truth_, _unlock_, _little-known_, _they don't want you to know_
- _Miraculous_, _supernatural breakthrough_ (when describing what the product delivers)
- _Anointed_ (when describing the product or the author)
- _God showed me_, _the Lord told me to write this_ (when used as a sales credential)
- _Seed_, _plant a seed_, _sow into_ (when describing the purchase price)

**Clichéd Christianese:**

- _On fire for God_
- _Walking in your calling / destiny / purpose_ (overused to the point of meaninglessness)
- _Breakthrough_, _breakthrough season_
- _Next level_, _level up your faith_
- _Spirit-filled life_ (vague; use specific language about what the Spirit does)
- _Hungry for more_
- _Doing life together_

**Performative warmth:**

- _Beloved_, _precious one_, _dear heart_
- _Sweet sister_, _brother in Christ_
- _I want to love on you_
- _Pouring into you_

**Vague spiritual claims:**

- _Transform your walk with God_ (too vague; specify what changes)
- _Deepen your relationship with Jesus_ (specify how)
- _Unleash God's power in your life_ (almost always false advertising)

**Generic marketing tropes that feel especially wrong in Christian copy:**

- _Game-changer_, _life hack_, _cheat code_
- _Insider_, _elite_, _exclusive_ (clash with Christian values of welcome)
- _Limited time only_ (when not literally true)
- _Act now or miss out forever_

**Words and Phrases That Work**

Several specific words and constructions consistently lift conversion in Christian copy because they signal authenticity, specificity, and biblical literacy without becoming jargon.

**Specific biblical language that works across denominations:**

- _Walk through_, _walking with_, _the road of..._
- _In this season_, _for this season_ (when not paired with "breakthrough")
- _The valley_, _the wilderness_, _the desert place_
- _Pressed_, _crushed_, _refined_, _shaped_
- _Quiet_, _still_, _steady_
- _Faithful_, _faithfulness_
- _Endure_, _endurance_

**Honest emotional language:**

- _Weight_, _the weight of it_
- _Heavy_, _bone-tired_, _worn down_
- _Quiet voice_, _the still small voice_ (used sparingly)
- _Long obedience_, _long road_
- _Hard season_, _the hard middle_

**Warm but non-performative direct address:**

- _You_, _your_, _you're_ (relentlessly)
- _If you've read this far_
- _You already know_
- _You don't need someone to tell you_

**Stewardship and value language:**

- _Worth_ (rather than "cost")
- _Investment in your soul_ (used once, not throughout)
- _Resource_, _companion_, _guide_ (for the product)
- _Walk through with you_ (for the product's function)

**Tone Calibration for the Hurting Reader**

Many Christian sales pages - and certainly any page on suffering, grief, anxiety, marital struggle, or spiritual dryness - are read by people in genuine pain. Their reading capacity is reduced. Their patience for hype is near zero. Their hunger for truth-telling is acute.

For these readers, several specific calibrations apply.

**Shorter sentences.** A hurting reader cannot track long, clause-heavy sentences. Cut the page's average sentence length by roughly 30% compared to a sales page written for a comfortable reader. Use sentence fragments at emotional peaks. They land harder. They give the reader breath.

**More white space.** Long unbroken paragraphs feel like more weight on someone already carrying weight. Break paragraphs at every natural pause. A page for the hurting reader has more paragraph breaks than a page for the curious browser.

**Acknowledge before promising.** The single most important calibration. Before any sentence that promises what the offer will do, the page must first acknowledge what the reader is currently experiencing. Promises without acknowledgment feel like the reader is being sold past. Acknowledgments without promises leave the reader stuck. The pattern is always: _I see where you are. Here is where this can take you._

**Lower the volume on urgency.** A reader in pain experiences urgency physically already; the page does not need to manufacture more. The pressure to act dials down, not up. Urgency for the hurting reader sounds like _whenever you're ready, this will be here_ paired with _and the longer you wait, the longer you walk this alone_. Both registers are present, but the first leads.

**More scripture, less marketing.** The ratio of biblical content to sales content rises when the reader is hurting. A page selling a productivity course can be 90% marketing and 10% scripture. A page selling a suffering bundle inverts that - scripture and biblical story carry the page, with marketing language appearing only at the structural seams.

**Permission to not buy.** This sounds counterintuitive but is essential. Somewhere in the page, the hurting reader needs to be told, plainly, that they are not obligated to buy and that the page will still be here when they're ready. This counterintuitive move raises conversion by removing pressure, which is what the hurting reader is most allergic to.

**The Author's Voice vs. the Skill's Default Voice**

This skill produces a baseline Christian voice that works across most authors and audiences. But every individual author has their own specific voice - their preferred phrases, their theological flavor, their rhythms, their way of opening and closing - and the final copy must sound like that author, not like generic Christian marketing.

The intake questionnaire captures enough of the author's voice to calibrate. Beyond that, templates/voice-calibration.md provides the detailed process for matching an individual author. The default voice in this file is the starting point, not the destination.

A useful test: if the author read the finished sales page aloud to their spouse, would the spouse recognize it as something the author would actually write? If yes, the voice is calibrated. If no, return to voice-calibration.md and re-tune.

**A Quick Reference Checklist**

Before delivering any draft, audit for:

The four pillars - warm, urgent, plain, second-person - present throughout.

The rhythm - _moment, anchor, return_ - appearing in major sections.

Brother / sister / friend used sparingly (zero to three times total) and never in the opening.

No words from the avoid list (manipulation triggers, clichéd Christianese, performative warmth, vague spiritual claims, generic marketing tropes).

Sentence length and paragraph length calibrated to the audience's emotional state (shorter for hurting readers, more standard for comfortable readers).

Acknowledgment present before every promise.

Permission to not buy stated somewhere in the page.

The author's specific voice recognizable, not just the skill's default voice.

**End of Voice Guide**

The voice is what makes the difference between a Christian sales page that converts and one that doesn't. Methodology gets the structure right. Voice gets the reader to keep reading. Both must be calibrated, and the voice is harder. Spend more time here than feels efficient. It pays.
