**The Section-by-Section Structure of a High-Converting Christian Sales Page**

**How to Use This Reference**

This is the structural backbone Claude uses when drafting any Christian sales page. Each section below explains what the section does, why it exists, which of the ten methods power it, how long it should run, and what mistakes to avoid.

A standard mid-ticket Christian sales page (\$47-\$197) uses every section here. Low-ticket pages collapse or skip several. High-ticket pages expand sections two, four, and seven significantly. The order below is the order they appear on the page - top to bottom, in the sequence the reader encounters them.

**Section 1 - The Hero Section (Above the Fold)**

**Purpose:** Stop the scroll, name the reader's situation, promise a specific transformation, and earn the next five seconds of attention.

**What goes here:**

- Pre-headline (optional, 4-7 words identifying the reader: _"For the Christian walking through suffering you didn't choose..."_)
- Main headline (the page's biggest promise, anchored in the Big Idea)
- Sub-headline (one sentence specifying the transformation and timeframe)
- Visual: stacked book covers, product mockup, or author photo
- Primary CTA button (visible without scrolling)

**Methods deployed:** Method 2 (Scripture-Anchored Big Idea), Method 4 (Transformation Bridge - its opening half)

**Length:** Roughly one screen on desktop, scannable in under five seconds.

**Common mistakes:** Cute headlines that prioritize cleverness over clarity. Vague promises like _"transform your life."_ Stock images of praying hands. Missing CTA above the fold.

**Conversion principle:** If the reader can't tell who this is for and what it promises within five seconds, the page has already lost them.

**Section 2 - The Empathy / Story Opening**

**Purpose:** Prove you understand the reader's pain before pitching the solution. Build trust through specificity and shared experience.

**What goes here:**

- Opening paragraph that names the exact feeling the reader carries
- Either a personal testimony (the author's own pain) or a representative reader's story
- Specific details that signal "I've actually been here"
- A turning-point moment that creates curiosity about what changed

**Methods deployed:** Method 1 (Testimony-Driven PAS - the Problem and Agitate phases), Method 7 (StoryBrand Hero's Journey - the hero meets their pain)

**Length:** 200-400 words. Long enough to land emotionally, short enough not to delay the offer.

**Common mistakes:** Generic suffering language ("we all go through hard times"). Author-centered storytelling that makes the reader feel like a spectator. Skipping straight to the solution without earning the right to offer it.

**Conversion principle:** Specificity is the currency of empathy. _"You've prayed the same prayer for eleven months and heard nothing"_ outperforms _"You're struggling with unanswered prayer"_ every time.

**Section 3 - The Problem Deepened**

**Purpose:** Show the reader the real cost of staying where they are. Not to manipulate - to clarify.

**What goes here:**

- The hidden cost of unaddressed pain (spiritual, relational, emotional)
- What happens to faith when suffering has no biblical framework
- The specific way isolation in pain compounds it
- One scripture validating the reader's experience (Psalm 34:18, Psalm 147:3, etc.)

**Methods deployed:** Method 1 (Testimony-Driven PAS - the Agitate phase), Method 8 (Awareness-Stage Layering - meeting the reader at their level of self-awareness)

**Length:** 150-300 words.

**Common mistakes:** Twisting the knife too long. Fear-mongering. Implying the reader is failing spiritually for struggling. Stacking suffering imagery without offering the turn.

**Conversion principle:** Agitation in Christian copy is not about making the reader feel worse - it's about helping them see clearly what they've been carrying alone.

**Section 4 - The Reframe / Big Idea Reveal**

**Purpose:** Introduce the contrarian biblical truth that reframes the reader's entire situation. This is the page's intellectual and spiritual pivot point.

**What goes here:**

- The Big Idea stated clearly in one bold sentence
- The scripture or biblical pattern it rests on (Joseph, Job, Paul, Jesus)
- A short explanation of why this changes everything
- A bridge sentence that connects the Big Idea to what the offer delivers

**Methods deployed:** Method 2 (Scripture-Anchored Big Idea - full deployment), Method 3 (Belief Breaking - first false belief addressed here)

**Length:** 200-350 words.

**Common mistakes:** Stating the Big Idea without backing it biblically. Backing it with a verse that's been stretched out of context. Making the reframe too abstract to be useful.

**Conversion principle:** The Big Idea is the spine of the entire page. Every section after this point either reinforces it, proves it, or applies it. If the Big Idea is weak, no value stack can save the page.

**Section 5 - Belief Breaking (The Three Walls)**

**Purpose:** Dismantle the specific false beliefs blocking the reader from saying yes. Without this section, even an excellent offer hits invisible walls.

**What goes here:**

- The three false beliefs the reader holds, named directly
  - **Vehicle belief:** _"Books / studies / bundles can't actually help with real pain"_
  - **Internal belief:** _"I don't have the faith to walk through this well"_
  - **External belief:** _"My situation is too unique / too far gone / too specific"_
- One short rebuttal per belief, ideally with scripture or a brief story
- A bridge into the offer reveal

**Methods deployed:** Method 3 (Belief Breaking - full deployment)

**Length:** 300-500 words. One paragraph per belief.

**Common mistakes:** Naming beliefs the reader doesn't actually hold. Spending too long on one belief and rushing the others. Using the section to brag about the offer instead of dismantling resistance.

**Conversion principle:** Most non-buyers don't reject the offer - they reject one of these three beliefs and never get to the offer. Break the wall and the offer sells itself.

**Section 6 - The Offer Reveal**

**Purpose:** Introduce what the reader is being invited into. Name it, frame it, and position it.

**What goes here:**

- The product name, said clearly
- A one-sentence positioning line (_"The complete 10-book journey for Christians walking through suffering they didn't choose"_)
- A visual: full bundle stack, mockup, or screenshot of what they get
- A bridge sentence into the value breakdown

**Methods deployed:** Method 4 (Transformation Bridge - the bridge revealed), Method 5 (Curated Library Frame, if applicable)

**Length:** 100-200 words. This is a reveal, not a deep dive - the value stack handles the depth.

**Common mistakes:** Burying the product name. Listing features instead of positioning. Showing the price here (price comes later).

**Conversion principle:** The reveal is a clean handoff: _"Here is the thing. Now let me show you exactly what's inside."_

**Section 7 - The Value Stack**

**Purpose:** Build perceived value section by section. By the time the reader sees the price, the value should feel obviously larger than the cost.

**What goes here:**

- Each component of the offer listed with:
  - Title
  - One-sentence description of what it addresses
  - Anchor scripture (if applicable)
  - Visual (book cover, module thumbnail)
  - Dollar value
- A running total
- Bonuses, if any, each with their own stacked value
- Final stacked value before price reveal

**Methods deployed:** Method 6 (Value Stack with Stewardship Framing - full deployment), Method 5 (Curated Library Frame - for library/membership offers)

**Length:** 600-1,200 words depending on how many components. This is the page's longest section by design.

**Common mistakes:** Inflated dollar values that strain credibility. Listing components without explaining the pain each one addresses. Skipping the visual for each book or module. Forgetting bonuses.

**Conversion principle:** The reader is silently asking _"is this worth it?"_ through the entire stack. Every component answers a different version of _"yes, because..."_

**Section 8 - Social Proof**

**Purpose:** Show the reader they are not alone in saying yes, and that real people have walked this path with real results.

**What goes here:**

- Testimonials (3-7 for mid-ticket, 7-15 for high-ticket)
- Specific transformations named, not generic praise
- Names, photos, and locations where possible
- Optionally: number of buyers, books distributed, ministry reach
- For new offers without testimonials: testimonials about the author's previous work, or about the source material

**Methods deployed:** Method 4 (Transformation Bridge - proof of the after-state), Method 7 (StoryBrand - proof that other heroes have succeeded with this guide)

**Length:** 300-600 words.

**Common mistakes:** Vague testimonials (_"This changed my life!"_ with no detail). All male or all female voices when the audience is mixed. Testimonials that praise the author instead of describing transformation. Fake-sounding testimonials - Christian readers detect these instantly.

**Conversion principle:** One specific testimonial (_"After chapter four, I prayed for the first time in eight months"_) outperforms ten generic ones.

**Section 9 - The Price Reveal and Stewardship Framing**

**Purpose:** Present the price in a way that feels like stewardship rather than expense.

**What goes here:**

- The stacked value total restated
- The actual price, clearly stated
- The framing line (_"Less than the cost of one Christian conference dinner"_ or _"Roughly \$9.70 per book"_)
- Payment plan if offered
- The CTA button

**Methods deployed:** Method 6 (Value Stack - the close of the stack), Method 10 (Risk Reversal + Kingdom Urgency - soft urgency introduced here)

**Length:** 150-300 words.

**Common mistakes:** Apologizing for the price. Over-explaining the discount math. Tithing language that feels coercive. Stacking too much urgency this early - the guarantee section handles that.

**Conversion principle:** The reader has already decided emotionally by this point. The price section just removes the last logical friction. Keep it clean.

**Section 10 - The Guarantee**

**Purpose:** Remove the risk of saying yes. Make the decision feel safe.

**What goes here:**

- A specific guarantee window (30 days, 60 days, etc.)
- The conditions, stated plainly
- The reasoning behind it (_"Because if this doesn't meet you where you are, I don't want your money"_)
- A guarantee badge or seal visual

**Methods deployed:** Method 10 (Risk Reversal + Kingdom Urgency - the reversal half)

**Length:** 100-200 words.

**Common mistakes:** Hedging the guarantee with conditions that feel slippery. Using legal language instead of pastoral language. Making the refund process sound difficult.

**Conversion principle:** A bold, simple guarantee converts higher than a clever conditional one. Christian buyers especially reward straightforward guarantees because so much Christian marketing has burned them.

**Section 11 - The Objection-Crushing FAQ**

**Purpose:** Kill the last remaining objections the reader is holding silently. Most buyers read the FAQ last before deciding.

**What goes here:**

- 6-12 questions covering the predictable objections:
  - _"Aren't your books free on the site? Why pay \$97?"_
  - _"What if I'm not a strong reader?"_
  - _"Is this prosperity gospel?"_
  - _"What denominations is this for?"_
  - _"What if my pain is \[specific situation\]?"_
  - _"How do I access the books?"_
  - _"Can I share this with my spouse / small group?"_
  - _"What if I've already read some of these?"_
- Plain, honest answers - no marketing voice here, just truth

**Methods deployed:** Method 9 (Objection-Crushing FAQ Close - full deployment)

**Length:** 400-800 words.

**Common mistakes:** Soft questions designed to sell instead of real objections the buyer is wrestling with. Marketing-voice answers that sound salesy. Avoiding the hardest objection (usually the "isn't it free elsewhere" question).

**Conversion principle:** The buyer who reads the FAQ is closer to yes than no. Treat this section as prime real estate, not filler.

**Section 12 - The Final Close and CTA**

**Purpose:** Land the emotional weight one last time and invite the decision.

**What goes here:**

- A short closing passage that names the choice the reader is facing
- The contrast between staying where they are and stepping into what the offer delivers
- Optionally one closing scripture (Hebrews 3:15, 2 Corinthians 6:2, James 1:22)
- A final, clear CTA button
- Optionally: signature line from the author with photo

**Methods deployed:** Method 4 (Transformation Bridge - the bridge crossed), Method 10 (Kingdom Urgency - full deployment), Method 7 (StoryBrand - the call to action moment)

**Length:** 200-400 words.

**Common mistakes:** Adding new arguments here (this is a close, not a new pitch). Manipulative scripture use. Fake countdown timers. Multiple CTAs that confuse the action.

**Conversion principle:** The final close mirrors the opening hook. Whatever pain or hunger the page opened with, the close names the doorway out of it.

**Section 13 - Post-Decision Reassurance (Optional)**

**Purpose:** Reassure the reader who is hovering over the button.

**What goes here:**

- A short paragraph addressing the still-uncertain reader
- A reminder of the guarantee
- A note that the author is reachable (_"Reply to any email - I read everything"_)
- The CTA repeated one final time

**Methods deployed:** Method 10 (Risk Reversal), Method 1 (Testimony - author's voice, one last time)

**Length:** 100-200 words.

**Common mistakes:** Repeating earlier sections. Sounding desperate. Adding a new urgency element.

**Conversion principle:** The reader who has scrolled this far is asking _"is it really safe?"_ Answer them quietly, then let them decide.

**How Section Length Scales by Price Point**

**Low-ticket (\$27 and under):** Collapse to sections 1, 2, 6, 7 (shortened), 9, 10, 11 (4-6 FAQs), 12. Total page length: 1,500-2,500 words.

**Mid-ticket (\$47-\$197):** Use all sections. Total page length: 3,500-5,500 words.

**High-ticket (\$297+):** Expand sections 2, 4, 5, 7, 8, 11. Add a methodology section between 4 and 5 explaining the system in depth. Total page length: 6,000-10,000 words.

**Upsell pages:** Compress sections 1, 4, 6, 7, 10, 12. Skip 3, 5, 11. The buyer has already said yes once - the upsell rides momentum, not first-time persuasion. Total page length: 1,200-2,500 words.

**Final Structural Principle**

A Christian sales page is not a feature list. It is a guided walk from where the reader is standing right now to the door of the offer. Every section moves the reader one step closer to the door. If a section doesn't move them, it doesn't belong on the page.

When in doubt, ask: _what does this section do for the reader's heart, mind, and decision?_ If the answer isn't immediate, cut it or rewrite it until it is.
