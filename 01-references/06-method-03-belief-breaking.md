**Method 03: Belief Breaking - The Three-Belief Framework**

**What This Method Is**

Every prospect arriving at a sales page is carrying three specific beliefs that, if left intact, will prevent the purchase. These beliefs are not objections in the traditional sense. Objections are conscious - the reader can name them and the FAQ can answer them. Beliefs are deeper. The reader holds them as background assumptions about how reality works, and they shape every other thought on the page without ever surfacing into clear language.

Dean Graziosi popularized the three-belief framework in his teaching and built it into the structure of nearly every sales page and webinar he produces. Russell Brunson teaches the same framework in slightly different language. The core insight predates both of them and goes back to the foundational direct response writers of the twentieth century: the sale does not happen until three specific belief barriers are dismantled.

The three beliefs are sometimes called the _vehicle_, _internal_, and _external_ beliefs. Each one blocks the sale in a distinct way, and each one requires a distinct kind of dismantling. A page that addresses one belief but leaves the others intact will convert poorly even if everything else is excellent. A page that addresses all three converts at rates that surprise the writer.

For Christian audiences, the three beliefs take on specific shapes that make this method particularly powerful. Christian readers have a long history of belief-formation around faith, suffering, transformation, and their own capacity for change. The beliefs blocking the sale are usually well-formed, deeply held, and rooted in years of teaching and experience. Breaking them requires more than a sentence - it requires a section, written with care, that respects the belief's origin and offers a genuine reframe.

This method is the conversion engine for the middle of most Christian sales pages. The opening (usually Method 01 or Method 02) establishes solidarity and presents the offer. Belief Breaking is what carries Stage 3 readers across the gap between interest and purchase.

**When to Use It**

Use Belief Breaking when:

- The audience is dominated by Stage 3 readers - solution-aware but skeptical that any specific solution will work for them
- The offer is mid-ticket or higher (\$47 and above), where the buying decision involves real evaluation
- The author can articulate the three specific beliefs blocking buyers from saying yes
- Previous offers in this category have failed or disappointed the audience, leaving residual skepticism
- The reader's current beliefs about their situation are demonstrably wrong or incomplete in ways the offer addresses

Do not use Belief Breaking when:

- The audience is overwhelmingly Stage 4 or 5 (product-aware, ready to buy) - they have already broken the beliefs and just need the path to checkout
- The author cannot clearly identify what the three blocking beliefs actually are (without specificity, this method produces vague filler)
- The offer is sub-\$27 and impulse-purchasable - the friction is not belief-shaped, it is just attention-shaped
- The three beliefs would require lengthy theological argument to break - in that case, the offer is probably wrong for this audience

For _The Purpose In Your Pain_ bundle, this method is essential. The audience is dominated by Stage 3 readers who have tried Christian books on suffering and been disappointed. Without explicit belief breaking, the bundle will be evaluated against those past disappointments and rejected.

**The Three Beliefs, Defined**

**Belief 1 - The Vehicle Belief**

The vehicle belief is the reader's conviction that the _category_ of solution being offered cannot help them. Not this specific product - the entire kind of thing it is.

For a Christian ebook bundle on suffering, the vehicle belief sounds like: _Books cannot help with what I am walking through. I have tried Christian books before. They are full of platitudes. The author writes from a comfortable life and tells me to trust God more, and that doesn't reach where I actually am._

For a Christian marriage course, the vehicle belief sounds like: _Online courses don't change marriages. My marriage needs counseling, not videos. Whatever this course teaches, my spouse won't watch it, and without both of us engaged, nothing changes._

For a Christian coaching program, the vehicle belief sounds like: _Coaching is for people with money to burn. The real spiritual work happens in church, in prayer, in scripture - not in calls with a stranger._

The vehicle belief is rooted in the reader's previous experiences with the category. They have tried books and been disappointed. They have signed up for courses and not finished. They have hired coaches who turned out to be glorified life coaches in Christian dress. The belief is empirical, in their experience. Breaking it requires showing them why this specific instance of the category is genuinely different - not by claiming it is different, but by demonstrating how.

**Belief 2 - The Internal Belief**

The internal belief is the reader's conviction that even if the offer is good, _they_ cannot use it well. The limitation is in them, not in the product.

For a Christian ebook bundle, the internal belief sounds like: _I don't have the discipline to read ten books. I start things and don't finish. My attention span is shot. I'll buy this and let it sit on my computer like all the other things I've bought._

For a Christian Bible study course, the internal belief sounds like: _I'm not smart enough for serious Bible study. I've never been good at theology. I'll be lost in the first week and embarrassed to admit it._

For a Christian business mentorship, the internal belief sounds like: _I don't have the kind of personality that succeeds at this. The people who do well are extroverts, salespeople, hustlers. I'm an introvert who would rather write than promote._

The internal belief is shame-shaped. The reader believes there is something deficient in them that will prevent the offer from working. They have evidence - every previous incomplete project, every abandoned plan, every season they started strong and faded. Breaking this belief requires removing the implied workload, reframing what success looks like, and either dignifying or transforming the trait they consider a liability.

**Belief 3 - The External Belief**

The external belief is the reader's conviction that their _circumstances_ are too unique or too unfavorable for the offer to work for them, regardless of the offer's quality or their own capacity.

For a Christian ebook bundle on suffering, the external belief sounds like: _My situation is too specific. The books in this bundle are written for normal suffering - grief, illness, marriage problems. What I'm walking through is different. Nobody in this bundle has walked my specific road._

For a Christian marriage course, the external belief sounds like: _My marriage is past the point where this could help. We have specific problems - abuse history, addiction, betrayal - that generic marriage content doesn't touch._

For a Christian financial course, the external belief sounds like: _My financial situation is too far gone. I don't have margin to apply this. I don't make enough. Whatever this teaches assumes a baseline I don't have._

The external belief is the most stubborn of the three because it has the most apparent legitimacy. The reader's circumstances _are_ specific. Their pain _is_ unique to them. Breaking this belief requires showing that specificity is the rule, not the exception - that the resources in the offer were created by people with their own specific circumstances, and that the framework being offered is robust enough to apply across specifics rather than requiring identical situations.

**The Structure**

A Belief Breaking section follows a consistent pattern, repeated three times for each of the three beliefs. The full section typically runs 600 to 1,200 words depending on the page length and the depth of resistance the audience carries.

**Step 1 - Surface the Belief in the Reader's Own Voice**

Begin each belief-break by naming the belief in the language the reader would use. Not the writer's interpretation of the belief. The reader's actual internal sentence.

This step is essential because the reader cannot break a belief they have not seen named. The moment the page names their belief accurately, the reader thinks _yes, that is exactly what I was thinking_, and the surface tension breaks. The belief is now external to them - they can examine it instead of inhabiting it.

Example for the vehicle belief: _You are probably reading this thinking: I have tried Christian books on suffering before. I bought three of them in 2022 and read maybe a chapter and a half before they ended up on the shelf. They all said the same things. Trust God. He's in control. There is purpose in pain. None of that helped me at 2 a.m. when the next wave hit. Why would these ten books be any different?_

The naming must be specific enough that the reader recognizes their own thought process. Generic phrases like "you might be skeptical" do not work. The internal sentence has to be reproduced with enough fidelity that the reader confirms it as theirs.

**Step 2 - Validate Why the Belief Exists**

Once the belief is named, the page validates it. The reader's reasons for holding the belief are real and good. Acknowledging this respects the reader and earns the right to challenge the conclusion.

Example: _That skepticism is earned. Most Christian books on suffering really are written by people who haven't suffered the way you have, or who package the experience as a tidy six-step process that breaks down the moment your actual life intrudes. The genre has a real problem. If you have been burned by it before, you have evidence - and the evidence is real, not paranoia._

This validation step is what separates Christian Belief Breaking from secular versions of the same method. Secular pages often mock the existing belief or treat it as obviously wrong. Christian readers reject that posture instantly. The validation must be sincere, and the reasons named must be the real reasons.

**Step 3 - Offer the Reframe Through Specific Evidence**

Now the page produces the evidence that the belief, while reasonable, is not fully accurate in this specific case. The evidence is concrete and verifiable, not promotional.

Example: _Here's what makes this bundle different. Each of these ten books was written by someone who walked through their own specific dark season and wrote the book they wished they'd had during it. Three of them lost children. Two of them walked through years of clinical depression. One was diagnosed with cancer at forty-one. One spent three years in a marriage that he eventually had to leave. These are not books on suffering written from comfortable lives. They are books on suffering written from the inside, by people who had nothing left to give the project except honesty._

The reframe earns the reader's openness by being specific and falsifiable. If the writer claims something the reader could check, and the claim holds, the reader's belief begins to bend.

**Step 4 - Make the Logical Connection Explicit**

Finally, the page connects the reframe back to the reader's situation. Now that the belief is dismantled, what does that mean for their decision today?

Example: _Which means the past disappointments you've had with the genre are not evidence about this bundle. They are evidence about a different category of books - the ones written from comfort rather than from the inside. If those left you cold, that doesn't predict what these will do. The thing you have been protecting yourself against is not what you are looking at right now._

This step closes the loop. The belief was named, validated, dismantled, and now its absence has consequences for the reader's pending decision. The reader is freer than they were a page ago.

**Three Worked Examples - All Three Beliefs in Sequence**

The examples below show full three-belief sections written for _The Purpose In Your Pain_ bundle, a Christian marriage course, and a Christian writing coaching program.

**Example A - The Purpose In Your Pain Bundle (Your Funnel)**

**Belief 1 - The Vehicle Belief**

_You are probably reading this thinking: I have tried Christian books on suffering before, and they didn't reach where I actually am. They told me to trust God and quoted Romans 8:28 and described their own three-month hard season as if it were equivalent to what I'm walking through. I read half a chapter and put the book down because the writer clearly had not been where I am._

_That skepticism is fair. Most Christian books on suffering really are written from a distance - by pastors who studied the problem rather than lived it, or by authors whose hard season ended on a deadline that worked for the publisher. The genre has a credibility problem, and you are right to be skeptical of it._

_Here's what makes this bundle different. Each of these ten books was written by someone who wrote from inside their own valley, not from the other side of it. Three of them buried children. One walked through eight years of clinical depression that no medication touched. One was diagnosed with a degenerative illness at thirty-six. One spent four years caring for a spouse with early-onset Alzheimer's. None of them wrote their book to package a tidy framework. They wrote because writing was the only way they could survive what they were walking through, and the books became maps almost by accident._

_Which means your past disappointments with the genre are not evidence about these books. They are evidence about books written from comfort. These are not those._

**Belief 2 - The Internal Belief**

_You are also probably reading this thinking: even if these books are different, I don't have the discipline to read ten of them. I am running on empty already. I open books and lose track of where I am. I buy things and let them sit. I'll buy this bundle and feel guilty in three weeks when I haven't started._

_That fear is reasonable. You are tired. Reading takes energy you don't have a lot of right now. And you have a track record of starting things and not finishing them, especially in this season, because everything in this season is harder than it used to be._

_Here's what to know. You don't read all ten of these books in a month. You don't read them in any particular order. You read the one that meets you where you are this week. Each book in the bundle has a short introduction that tells you what season of suffering it was written for - the early shock, the grinding middle, the unexpected anger, the slow rebuilding, the moments of grace. You pick the one that matches your week. You read it slowly. You let it sit. When you're ready, you pick another. The bundle is built for someone who is tired, not for someone with discipline to spare._

_Which means the discipline you don't have is not the discipline this bundle requires._

**Belief 3 - The External Belief**

_The third thing you are probably thinking is: my situation is too specific for this. The books in this bundle were written for grief, or illness, or marriage trouble, or whatever - but what I'm walking through is its own thing. Nobody in this bundle wrote about my exact road._

_That sense of specificity is real. Your suffering is yours. Nobody else has walked your road in your shoes. That is true and it deserves to be honored._

_Here is what scripture and these books both demonstrate. Every figure in the Bible who walked through suffering walked through their own specific version of it. Joseph's prison was not Job's ash heap. Hannah's barrenness was not Paul's thorn. David's caves were not Elijah's juniper tree. The specificity is the rule, not the exception. And yet there is a framework underneath all of them - a way of carrying pain with God in the middle of it, on God's timeline, that recurs across every story in scripture. These ten books teach that framework. The framework does not require your suffering to look like the author's. It applies to your specific road because the framework is what scripture demonstrates across radically different specifics._

_Which means the uniqueness of your situation is not a disqualifier. It is the rule. The framework is what works across the rule._

**Example B - A Christian Marriage Course**

**Belief 1 - Vehicle**

_You are probably reading this thinking: my marriage doesn't need a course. It needs counseling. Maybe an intervention. Whatever this teaches, my spouse won't watch the videos, and without both of us engaged, nothing changes._

_That instinct is right in one direction and wrong in another. You're right that a course can't fix what counseling addresses - patterns that require a trained professional to surface and treat. If your marriage has those patterns, get counseling. This course doesn't replace that._

_What this course does is something counseling generally doesn't. It rebuilds the spiritual architecture of a marriage - the prayer life, the scripture rhythm, the shared sense of what the marriage is for under God - that counseling assumes is already in place. Most Christian marriages that struggle don't lack communication skills. They lack a shared spiritual frame. Counseling can't install one. This course can._

_Which means counseling and this course are not competing options. They address different layers. If your marriage needs both, get both. If the layer that's actually broken is the spiritual one, this is the layer this addresses._

**Belief 2 - Internal**

_The second thing you're probably thinking: I'm not the one who needs to change. My spouse is. I can't make them watch the videos, and I shouldn't have to do the work for both of us._

_That frustration is legitimate. The asymmetry is real. One spouse usually wakes up wanting to fix things before the other does. That is the shape of almost every Christian marriage that eventually heals - one person started the work alone._

_Here's what to know. This course is designed for the spouse who shows up first. The first half is built specifically for someone working on the marriage without their partner's initial engagement. You do not need your spouse to watch any of it for the early modules to shift what you bring into the marriage daily. By the time the later modules come into play - the ones that work better when both spouses participate - something has usually shifted in the dynamic enough that the second spouse becomes curious. Not always. But often enough that this design has held up across hundreds of marriages._

**Belief 3 - External**

_The third thing: my marriage has specific problems that generic Christian marriage content doesn't address. There is history here. There is betrayal, or addiction, or family-of-origin stuff that runs deep. A course made for normal marriages isn't going to touch what we're walking through._

_The specificity of your situation is real. Every hard marriage has its own particular wounds. That deserves acknowledgment._

_And - the framework this course teaches is not for "normal" marriages. It is for marriages with weight in them. The case studies inside the course include marriages working through addiction recovery, marriages rebuilding after betrayal, marriages navigating chronic illness, marriages where one spouse came to faith later than the other. The framework was built for marriages that have been through it, not for marriages that haven't been tested. The specifics of your situation are not exceptions to this course's reach. They are the kinds of situations the course was built around._

**Example C - A Christian Writing Coaching Program**

**Belief 1 - Vehicle**

_You are probably thinking: coaching programs are mostly hype. They sell a transformation that doesn't deliver. The people who succeed would have succeeded anyway, and the program just took credit. I've seen friends spend thousands on programs and end up exactly where they started._

_That pattern is real. Most coaching programs underdeliver, especially in the Christian space, where the bar has been particularly low. You have evidence for the skepticism._

_Here is what this program is not. It is not a motivation program, a mindset program, or a "manifest your calling" program. It is a craft program. Twelve weeks of specific instruction on the mechanics of Christian writing - sentence rhythm, scriptural integration, narrative structure, the discipline of a daily word count. You finish the program with a completed manuscript draft, not with motivation. The deliverable is concrete and demonstrable._

**Belief 2 - Internal**

_The second thing: I'm not actually a writer. I've never published anything. The people in this program will be further along than me, and I'll feel behind the whole time._

_That fear is understandable. Christian writing spaces can feel like everyone has a platform already and you're the only one starting from zero. The comparison loop is real._

_Here's what to know. The cohort is structured so beginners and experienced writers work on different deliverables. You are not compared to anyone. You are compared to where you were last week. And the most consistent outcome from the program is from writers who entered it never having finished a chapter - because the program teaches the system that produces the chapter, not the talent that doesn't need a system._

**Belief 3 - External**

_The third thing: my life doesn't have space for this. Between work, kids, and church, I can't add twelve weeks of coaching._

_The bandwidth concern is real. You are not wrong about your calendar._

_The program is designed for working parents. The time commitment is four hours per week including the live call. The writing is structured to happen in twenty-minute increments, six days a week, scheduled around your existing rhythm rather than competing with it. If your life truly has zero margin, this won't fit. If you have twenty minutes a day and four hours on Wednesdays, this fits._

**Common Mistakes**

**Mistake 1 - Naming Beliefs That Are Not the Real Beliefs**

The most frequent failure is naming generic beliefs instead of the actual specific beliefs blocking the audience. _"You might be skeptical"_ is not a belief. It is a hedge. The reader's actual internal sentence is specific, detailed, and rooted in past experience. If the writer cannot name it precisely, the belief-break does not land - because the reader does not recognize their own thought process in the writing.

The fix is to interview the audience before writing. Real customers, real conversations, real language. The beliefs are sitting in the audience's voice. The writer's job is to find them and quote them back.

**Mistake 2 - Mocking the Belief Instead of Validating It**

When the writer treats the existing belief as foolish or obviously wrong, the reader reflexively defends it. The belief now has emotional weight it did not have before, and the reframe cannot get through.

Christian readers especially reject contempt for their reasoning. The validation step is not optional. Even when the belief is empirically incorrect, the reader had reasons for forming it. Those reasons get named with respect before they get reframed.

**Mistake 3 - Reframing Without Specific Evidence**

A reframe that asserts "this is different" without showing why is not a reframe. It is a marketing claim. The reader compares the claim against their existing belief, sees no new evidence, and dismisses it.

Strong reframes contain specific, verifiable evidence. Names of authors. Numbers of years. Particular circumstances. Falsifiable claims. The specificity is what produces the belief-bend. Vague assurances do not.

**Mistake 4 - Addressing Only One or Two Beliefs**

Writers often address the vehicle belief well, gesture at the internal belief, and skip the external belief entirely - usually because the external belief is the hardest to break. Skipping any of the three leaves the sale incomplete.

The reader can carry one un-broken belief out of the page and still buy. The reader cannot carry two and buy. If only one belief is being broken cleanly, conversion stays low regardless of the rest of the page.

**Mistake 5 - Breaking Beliefs in the Wrong Order**

The natural sequence is vehicle, internal, external. The vehicle belief is broken first because the reader cannot evaluate their own capacity to use a product they still believe is a fraud. The internal belief is broken second because the reader needs to believe they personally can succeed before specifics become relevant. The external belief is broken third because it is the most situation-specific and benefits from being addressed after the foundational beliefs have shifted.

Reversing the order makes each belief harder to break than it should be. Writers who lead with the external belief - "you might think your situation is too specific" - find that the reader has not yet conceded that any solution in this category could work for anyone, which is a prior belief that must shift first.

**Mistake 6 - Belief Breaking Becomes the Whole Page**

A page can spend so much time breaking beliefs that the offer itself never gets fully introduced. The reader walks away convinced the writer understands them but unclear what they are actually being offered.

Belief Breaking is a middle-of-page section, not the entire page. It usually runs 600 to 1,200 words across all three beliefs. If it is running longer, the writer is overworking the section, and the page is losing momentum on the offer reveal.

**How This Method Combines With Others**

Belief Breaking is the workhorse of the middle of a Christian sales page. It almost never opens the page or closes it, but it does heavy lifting between the testimony and the offer.

**With Method 01 (Testimony-Driven PAS):** Testimony opens the page and establishes solidarity. Belief Breaking follows directly, addressing the resistance that surfaces in the reader once the offer is implied. This is the most common sequence on Christian sales pages and works reliably.

**With Method 02 (Scripture-Anchored Big Idea):** The Big Idea is itself a meta-level belief-break - it reframes the reader's overarching belief about their situation. The three-belief section sits underneath the Big Idea, addressing the secondary beliefs that survive the reframe.

**With Method 04 (Transformation Bridge):** After Belief Breaking, the page often pivots into Transformation Bridge - showing the before-and-after that becomes possible now that the beliefs are dismantled. The sequence is: break the belief that change is possible, then show what change looks like.

**With Method 06 (Value Stack):** The value stack often does light belief-reinforcement work for the internal belief. Each item in the stack is small enough to feel manageable, which reinforces the reframe that the reader can use this offer well.

**With Method 09 (Objection-Crushing FAQ):** Beliefs and objections are different layers - beliefs are background assumptions, objections are surface-level concerns. The FAQ handles objections after Belief Breaking has handled beliefs. Both are needed. The FAQ alone cannot reach the deeper beliefs, and Belief Breaking alone cannot address the practical objections.

Belief Breaking pairs poorly with very short sales pages. If the page is under 1,500 words, there is not enough space to break three beliefs well. In that case, the page should pick the single most blocking belief and break it deeply, leaving the other two for follow-up emails or the post-purchase sequence.

**End of Method 03**

The three beliefs are sitting in the reader's mind whether the page addresses them or not. A page that names them and dismantles them is doing the work the reader cannot do for themselves. A page that ignores them is leaving the reader to argue silently with the page until they close the tab. The first kind of page converts. The second kind doesn't. Belief Breaking is the difference.
