**Step-by-Step Process to Generate 20 Headlines and Pick the Top 3**

**Why This Process Exists**

The headline carries roughly 80% of the conversion weight of a sales page. Every other section can be excellent and the page will still underperform if the headline fails to stop the scroll and earn the next five seconds.

Gary Halbert taught generations of copywriters to write 20-50 headlines before picking one. The reason is not that the 47th headline is magically better than the first - it's that the first three or four headlines are almost always lazy. They reach for the obvious framing, the comfortable phrasing, the safe promise. The breakthrough headlines emerge somewhere between #8 and #20, after the obvious options have been exhausted and the writer's mind is forced into fresher territory.

This template walks Claude through that process specifically for Christian sales pages. The output is not "a headline" - it is 20 candidates, narrowed to 3 finalists, with one recommended choice and reasoning. The user makes the final pick, but Claude does the heavy lifting.

**Step 1 - Pull the Headline Inputs from Intake**

Before generating headlines, Claude assembles the specific inputs that good headlines require. These come from the intake questionnaire answers.

**Required inputs:**

- **The reader identity** (intake 3.1) - _"the Christian walking through suffering you didn't choose"_
- **The specific pain or aspiration** (intake 4.1) - _"feeling like prayer is talking to the ceiling"_
- **The Big Idea** (intake 6.3) - _"your pain isn't punishment, it's preparation"_
- **The anchor scripture** (intake 6.1) - _Genesis 50:20, Romans 8:28_
- **The transformation** (intake 7.1) - _"a biblical framework for suffering and scripture memorized for the hardest moments"_
- **The offer specifics** (intake 1.1, 1.4) - _10-book bundle, \$97_
- **The biblical figure parallel, if any** (intake 6.2) - _Joseph, Job, Paul_
- **The author's voice** (intake 5.3) - _warm and pastoral / direct and challenging / gentle and meditative_

If any of these are missing, Claude pauses and gathers them before generating headlines. Generating 20 headlines without these inputs produces 20 vague headlines.

**Step 2 - Generate Headlines Across the Eight Proven Formulas**

Christian sales pages convert best when the headline uses one of eight proven formulas. Claude generates 2-3 candidates per formula, producing roughly 20 total. The formulas are below, each with structure and 2 example outputs for a Christian suffering bundle to show the pattern.

**Formula 1 - Identity + Promise**

Names the reader directly, then promises a specific transformation.

**Structure:** _"For the \[READER IDENTITY\] \[WHO IS DOING/FEELING X\]: \[SPECIFIC PROMISE\]"_

**Examples:**

- _"For the Christian Walking Through Suffering You Didn't Choose: A Biblical Framework for Every Step Ahead"_
- _"For Believers Who've Prayed the Same Prayer for Months Without an Answer: Scripture That Meets You in the Silence"_

**When this works:** When the reader avatar is sharply defined and the buyer recognizes themselves instantly. High-converting for problem-aware audiences.

**Formula 2 - Big Idea Reframe**

States the contrarian biblical truth that reframes the reader's situation.

**Structure:** _"Your \[PAIN/SITUATION\] Isn't \[ASSUMED MEANING\] - It's \[BIG IDEA REFRAME\]"_

**Examples:**

- _"Your Suffering Isn't Punishment - It's Preparation"_
- _"The Silence in Your Prayer Life Isn't God's Absence - It's the Most Important Conversation You're Not Hearing"_

**When this works:** When the Big Idea is genuinely contrarian and emotionally loaded. The strongest single formula for high-converting Christian pages.

**Formula 3 - Question Hook**

Opens with a question the reader has been silently asking.

**Structure:** _"What If \[PAIN\] Was Actually \[REFRAME\]?"_ or _"Why Does \[SITUATION\] Feel Like \[FEELING\]?"_

**Examples:**

- _"What If Your Suffering Was Preparing You for Something Only Broken People Can Do?"_
- _"Why Does God Feel Silent When You Need Him Most? (And What Joseph Knew That You Probably Don't)"_

**When this works:** When the reader's internal monologue is dominated by a specific question. Risky if the question is too generic.

**Formula 4 - Scripture-Anchored**

Builds the headline directly around an anchor scripture or biblical figure.

**Structure:** _"\[SCRIPTURE PARAPHRASE OR REFERENCE FRAME\] - And How \[N\] Books Walk You Through It"_

**Examples:**

- _"'You Intended It for Harm, But God Intended It for Good' - The 10-Book Journey Through Christian Suffering"_
- _"What Joseph Learned in Thirteen Years of Prison - And How It Reshapes Your Hardest Season"_

**When this works:** When the anchor scripture is recognizable and emotionally loaded. Avoid if the verse is obscure or requires explanation to land.

**Formula 5 - Specificity-Driven**

Leads with concrete numbers, timelines, or specifics that signal substance.

**Structure:** _"The \[N\]-\[FORMAT\] \[JOURNEY\] for Christians \[SPECIFIC SITUATION\]"_

**Examples:**

- _"The 10-Book Journey for Christians Walking Through Suffering - Read at Your Pace, Anchored in Scripture"_
- _"30 Days, 10 Books, One Biblical Framework for the Pain You Didn't Choose"_

**When this works:** When the offer's specifics are themselves impressive. Strong for buyers who distrust vague promises and reward concrete substance.

**Formula 6 - Curiosity Gap (Christian Voice)**

Plants a question or partial revelation the reader needs to resolve.

**Structure:** _"The \[SCRIPTURE/PRINCIPLE/BIBLICAL FIGURE\] That Most Christians Miss About \[TOPIC\]"_

**Examples:**

- _"The Verse That Reframes Suffering as Preparation - And the 10-Book Journey Built Around It"_
- _"Why Joseph's Prison Years Matter More Than His Palace Years (And What That Means for Your Hardest Season)"_

**When this works:** When the curiosity is genuine, not click-bait. Christian audiences punish curiosity gaps that overpromise and underdeliver.

**Formula 7 - Before / After Contrast**

Names the reader's current state and the promised future state in one line.

**Structure:** _"From \[BEFORE STATE\] to \[AFTER STATE\]: \[METHOD/OFFER\]"_

**Examples:**

- _"From 'Where Is God in This?' to 'I Know Exactly Where He's Been All Along': The 10-Book Journey Through Suffering"_
- _"From Prayer That Feels Like Talking to the Ceiling to Prayer That Feels Like Coming Home: A Scripture-Anchored Path"_

**When this works:** When both states are vivid and specific. Weaker when either state is generic.

**Formula 8 - Negation / Pattern Interrupt**

Opens by negating what the reader expects to hear, creating attention.

**Structure:** _"This Isn't Another \[CATEGORY OF THING\]. It's \[WHAT IT ACTUALLY IS\]."_

**Examples:**

- _"This Isn't Another Christian Book About Suffering. It's the Journey Through It."_
- _"You Don't Need More Sermons on Pain. You Need the Books That Walk You Through It."_

**When this works:** When the reader has been burned by the obvious-category version of the offer (more sermons, more devotionals, more platitudes). Risky for audiences who haven't been burned - the negation lands flat.

**Step 3 - Generate the Full 20**

Claude works through the formulas systematically, producing 2-3 candidates per formula. The output before narrowing should look like a single block of 20 headlines, numbered 1-20, with no commentary yet. Each headline stands on its own.

Claude does NOT skip formulas because they "don't feel right." The point of generating across all eight is to force the mind out of its default groove. The best headline often comes from the formula that felt least natural to start with.

If a formula produces an obviously weak candidate, Claude generates it anyway and moves on. The narrowing in Step 4 is where weak candidates die.

**Step 4 - Apply the Five-Test Narrowing Process**

After generating 20 headlines, Claude narrows to the top 5 using these five tests. Each headline either passes or fails each test. The top 5 are the ones that pass the most tests, with priority given to tests 1, 2, and 5.

**Test 1 - The Five-Second Test**

Read the headline as if you'd never seen it before. In five seconds or less, can you answer these three questions?

- _Who is this for?_
- _What does it promise?_
- _Why should I care?_

Headlines that fail any of these three questions in five seconds get cut. Christian readers on phones don't slow down for ambiguous headlines.

**Test 2 - The Specificity Test**

Compare the headline to a vague-but-grammatical version of the same idea. Does the actual headline contain specific words that the vague version doesn't?

_Vague: "Find Hope in Your Suffering Through This Christian Book Bundle"_ _Specific: "Your Suffering Isn't Punishment - It's Preparation: The 10-Book Journey Through Christian Pain"_

The specific version names a contrarian truth (not punishment, preparation), names the format (10-book journey), and names the category (Christian pain). The vague version could be written by anyone for anything. Cut the vague ones.

**Test 3 - The Voice Test**

Read the headline out loud. Does it sound like something the author would actually say to a friend over coffee? Or does it sound like marketing language pretending to be Christian?

Headlines that read as marketing-voice rather than author-voice get cut, even if they're technically clever. Voice mismatch on a headline tanks the entire page.

**Test 4 - The Conversion-Killer Audit**

Run the headline against references/conversion-killers.md. Specifically check for:

- Hype words (INCREDIBLE, AMAZING, REVOLUTIONARY)
- Manufactured urgency language (LAST CHANCE, ONLY TODAY)
- Prosperity-gospel framing (YOUR BREAKTHROUGH IS HERE)
- "Secrets" framing (THE SECRET THE CHURCH WON'T TELL YOU)
- Inflated promises (END YOUR SUFFERING FOREVER)

Any match triggers an automatic cut.

**Test 5 - The Honest Promise Test**

Ask: _If a buyer read only this headline and nothing else, then purchased - would they get what the headline promised?_

If the answer is yes, the headline passes. If the answer is "they'd get something close but not exactly what the headline implied," the headline gets cut. Christian buyers tolerate honest underselling. They punish overselling, even subtle overselling.

**Step 5 - Narrow Top 5 to Top 3**

After applying the five tests, Claude has 5 surviving headlines. Now Claude narrows to the final 3 using a different lens: variety.

The goal is to present 3 headlines that represent _different strategic angles_ on the same offer, so the user can choose based on what they want the page to emphasize.

**Recommended final mix:**

- **One Big Idea headline** (Formula 2) - leads with the contrarian truth
- **One Identity + Promise headline** (Formula 1) - leads with the reader and the transformation
- **One Specificity or Scripture headline** (Formula 5 or 4) - leads with concrete substance or biblical anchor

If the surviving 5 don't include one from each angle, Claude returns to Step 2 and generates 2-3 more in the missing angle, then re-narrows.

**Step 6 - Recommend One Headline with Reasoning**

After delivering the top 3, Claude recommends one of them as the strongest choice and explains why.

**Recommendation format:**

**My recommendation: Headline \[N\] - "\[HEADLINE TEXT\]"**

Reasoning: \[2-4 sentences explaining why this headline is the strongest fit, based on the reader avatar, the awareness stage, the Big Idea strength, and the author's voice.\]

**Why not the others:**

- Headline \[X\] is strong but \[SPECIFIC REASON it's slightly weaker for this offer\]
- Headline \[Y\] is strong but \[SPECIFIC REASON\]

The user makes the final call, but Claude's recommendation gives them a clear default and the reasoning to argue with if they prefer another option.

**Step 7 - Generate the Sub-Headline**

Every main headline needs a sub-headline that specifies the transformation and timeframe. After the user picks the main headline, Claude generates 3 sub-headline candidates.

**Sub-headline structure:**

_"\[CONCRETE BEFORE STATE\] → \[CONCRETE AFTER STATE\] in \[TIMEFRAME\] through \[METHOD\]"_

**Examples for a suffering bundle:**

- _"Trade prayer that feels like talking to the ceiling for prayer rooted in the same scriptures Joseph, Job, and Paul stood on - over the next 30 days, one book at a time."_
- _"A 10-book journey that walks you from 'where is God in this?' to 'I know exactly where He's been all along' - at your own pace, anchored in scripture."_
- _"Curated biblical companionship for the Christian walking through suffering they didn't choose - read on your phone, your tablet, or your laptop, wherever you are."_

The user picks one, and the headline + sub-headline pair becomes the page's opening.

**What the Final Output Looks Like**

When Claude finishes the headline generation process, the user receives:

- **The full 20 headlines**, numbered, with the formula labeled on each so the user can see the range.
- **The top 3 headlines**, with the variety lens noted (Big Idea / Identity + Promise / Specificity or Scripture).
- **Claude's recommended pick**, with reasoning.
- **3 sub-headline candidates** for the recommended (or user-chosen) headline.

Total time for this process when intake is complete: about 5 minutes of Claude's drafting, no back-and-forth required unless the user wants different angles.

**Common Failure Modes**

**Generating fewer than 20 because "the first ones are already good."** The whole point of generating 20 is to force past the obvious framings. Stop at 7 and Claude almost always picks something serviceable instead of something strong.

**Treating the formulas as rigid templates rather than starting points.** The formulas are scaffolding. The actual headline often modifies the formula significantly. The point is to use the formula to generate the candidate, then refine.

**Forgetting to test each headline against the conversion-killer audit.** A clever headline that uses hype language or fake urgency still has to be cut. The audit is non-negotiable.

**Recommending without reasoning.** Telling the user "I recommend Headline 7" without explaining why leaves them to choose blindly. The reasoning is what makes the recommendation useful.

**Generating sub-headlines that just repeat the headline in different words.** The sub-headline's job is to _add specificity_ - the transformation, the timeframe, the method. If the sub-headline is a rephrase of the headline, it's not doing its job.

**The Underlying Principle**

The headline is the door the entire page hangs from. A weak headline closes the door before the reader sees what's behind it. A strong headline opens the door and pulls the reader through.

The 20-headline process is not about quantity for its own sake - it's about giving the writer enough creative friction to find the headline that actually opens the door. The 18th headline isn't magically better than the 3rd. The process of writing the 3rd, 8th, 12th, and 18th is what teaches the writer what the door actually looks like.

When in doubt about which headline to recommend, ask: _if a believer in genuine pain saw only this one line, would they want to read the next sentence?_ If the answer is unambiguously yes, the headline is doing its job. If the answer is "maybe, depending on mood," go back to the drawing board.
