**Matching an Author's Voice, Theology, and Denomination**

**Why Voice Calibration Decides Whether the Page Converts**

Generic Christian marketing copy reads as generic because it sounds like it could have been written for any Christian author by any Christian copywriter. The vocabulary is interchangeable, the cadence is predictable, and the theology is so safely middle-of-the-road that it triggers no recognition in readers who know the specific author.

A sales page that converts well does the opposite. The reader recognizes the author's voice in the first paragraph. The vocabulary, the rhythm, the theological emphases, and the small linguistic tells all align with what the reader knows from the author's books, social posts, or sermons. The page reads as if the author wrote it personally - even when Claude wrote it.

This is voice calibration. Done well, it converts because readers trust voices they recognize. Done poorly, the page reads as marketing dressed up in the author's name, and conversion suffers even when every other element is excellent.

This template walks Claude through the four-dimensional calibration process: tone, vocabulary, theology, and denomination. Each dimension has specific inputs to gather, specific patterns to match, and specific failure modes to avoid.

**Dimension 1 - Tone**

Tone is the emotional posture the author takes toward the reader. It is the most immediately felt dimension and the easiest to get wrong by defaulting to generic warmth.

**Step 1.1 - Identify the Author's Dominant Tone**

Most Christian authors fall predominantly into one of seven tone categories. Some blend two, but one is usually dominant. Claude identifies the dominant tone from intake answer 5.3 and from any author writing samples provided.

**Pastoral / shepherding** - warm, gentle, protective of the reader, often using phrases like "friend," "let me walk with you," "you are not alone." Authors like Henri Nouwen, Tim Keller in his more pastoral moments, John Eldredge.

**Direct / challenging** - confrontational in service of growth, uses imperatives, names hard truths, refuses to soften. Authors like Francis Chan, David Platt, Eric Mason, parts of Lecrae's writing.

**Meditative / contemplative** - slow, image-rich, asks questions rather than asserting, comfortable with mystery. Authors like Richard Rohr, parts of A.W. Tozer, contemplative voices.

**Prophetic / bold** - declarative, anointing-aware, comfortable with bold spiritual claims, often charismatic-adjacent. Authors like Bill Johnson, Heidi Baker, Todd White.

**Teacher / expositional** - precise, definitional, builds arguments step by step, comfortable with scripture exegesis. Authors like John Piper, R.C. Sproul, Tim Keller in his teaching mode.

**Friend / conversational** - casual, self-deprecating, uses humor, sounds like a coffee-shop conversation. Authors like Jen Hatmaker, Bob Goff, parts of Annie F. Downs.

**Storyteller / narrative** - leads with story, builds principles inside narrative, uses scene and dialogue. Authors like Donald Miller, Philip Yancey, Brennan Manning.

If the user provides writing samples in intake 5.3, Claude reads them and identifies which category fits best. If no samples are provided, Claude asks for one - a paragraph from one of the author's books, a recent social post, or a sample email. Voice cannot be calibrated without a sample.

**Step 1.2 - Match Cadence and Sentence Rhythm**

Beyond tone category, each author has a characteristic sentence rhythm. Claude reads the sample for three patterns:

**Sentence length pattern** - Does the author write predominantly short, punchy sentences (Francis Chan)? Long, layered sentences (Tim Keller)? Alternating short and long for rhythm (Henri Nouwen)? Match the page's sentence length distribution to the author's.

**Paragraph length pattern** - Does the author write one-sentence paragraphs for emphasis? Three-to-four sentence paragraphs as a default? Long, dense paragraphs that build arguments? Match the page's paragraph length to the author's.

**Rhetorical patterns** - Does the author use rhetorical questions frequently? Anaphora (repeated sentence openings)? Direct address to the reader ("you")? Specific transition phrases ("here's the thing," "but here's what I've learned," "listen,")? Catalog these and use them.

**Step 1.3 - Avoid the Tone Drift to Generic**

The most common voice failure is tone drift - the page starts in the author's voice and gradually drifts toward generic Christian marketing voice as it goes. This happens because generic voice is easier to write and Claude's default when not actively calibrating.

To prevent drift, Claude re-reads the author's sample after every section is drafted and asks: _does this section sound like the author or like generic Christian copy?_ Sections that drift get rewritten.

**Step 1.4 - Tone Failure Modes**

**Over-warming.** Defaulting to pastoral warmth for every author, including authors whose actual voice is direct, prophetic, or teacherly. A direct-voice author whose page reads pastoral comes across as inauthentic.

**Under-warming.** Defaulting to direct or teacherly voice for authors whose actual tone is pastoral or conversational. A pastoral author's page that reads direct comes across as harsh and off-brand.

**Tone uniformity across sections.** Some sections of a sales page naturally call for different micro-tones - the empathy section is warmer than the value stack, the FAQ is more direct than the close. But the underlying author voice should remain consistent. The micro-tones shift; the voice doesn't.

**Dimension 2 - Vocabulary**

Vocabulary is the specific word choices that signal authorial fingerprint. Two pages can have identical structure and tone yet feel completely different because of vocabulary.

**Step 2.1 - Catalog the Author's Signature Words**

Every Christian author has 10-30 signature words or phrases that appear in their work repeatedly. These are the words that, if a reader heard them out of context, would make them think of this specific author.

From the writing sample, Claude identifies:

**Theological vocabulary the author uses** - _grace, mercy, sanctification, formation, discipleship, kingdom, anointing, breakthrough, abiding, surrender._ Each tradition and each author favors specific terms.

**Metaphorical vocabulary the author uses** - _journey, path, valley, mountain, garden, table, wilderness, doorway, threshold._ Authors tend to return to a small set of metaphors repeatedly.

**Casual vocabulary the author uses** - _friend, brother, sister, beloved, listen, look, here's the thing._ These small words signal voice strongly.

**Words the author avoids** - Some authors never use _crusade, warrior, blessed, anointed._ Others never use _journey, story, narrative._ Note the absences as much as the presences.

**Step 2.2 - Use Signature Vocabulary in the Page**

Claude weaves the author's signature vocabulary into the page naturally, not as a checklist. The goal is for the reader to feel the author's vocabulary present without noticing it consciously.

A useful technique: pick 5-10 signature words from the catalog and deliberately work them into the page at natural points. If the author uses _formation_ often, the value stack might describe books as _resources for formation_ rather than _resources for growth._ If the author uses _table_ as a recurring metaphor, the close might invite the reader to _come to the table_ rather than _take the next step._

**Step 2.3 - Match Vocabulary Register**

Each author writes at a specific vocabulary register - the average reading level and sophistication of their word choices.

**High register** - uses _eschatology, sanctification, hermeneutics, theologically robust_ without explanation. Authors like Tim Keller, John Piper, R.C. Sproul.

**Mid register** - uses theological vocabulary but defines or contextualizes it. Authors like Henri Nouwen, Philip Yancey, parts of John Eldredge.

**Low register** - avoids theological jargon, uses plain language, writes for accessibility. Authors like Bob Goff, Jen Hatmaker, parts of Annie F. Downs.

Claude matches the register of the sales page to the register of the author. A high-register author's page can use _formation, sanctification, eschatological hope_ without explanation. A low-register author's page should use _growth, becoming more like Jesus, hope for what's coming_ instead.

**Step 2.4 - Vocabulary Failure Modes**

**Vocabulary stuffing.** Forcing 20 signature words into the page so densely that it reads as parody. Calibration is about presence, not concentration. 5-10 well-placed signature words outperform 20 stuffed ones.

**Wrong-register vocabulary.** Using _eschatological hope_ on a Bob Goff-voiced page, or using _the journey of becoming more like Jesus_ on a John Piper-voiced page. Register mismatch is detected instantly by readers familiar with the author.

**Default theological vocabulary.** Defaulting to generic Christian marketing vocabulary (_breakthrough, victory, abundant life, your best self_) regardless of the author's actual word choices. This is the fastest path to generic copy.

**Dimension 3 - Theology**

Theology is the substantive doctrinal positioning of the page. Voice without theological alignment produces copy that sounds like the author but says things the author wouldn't actually say.

**Step 3.1 - Identify the Author's Theological Lane**

From intake 5.4 and any writing samples, Claude identifies the author's primary theological lane. Most Christian authors operate within one of these lanes:

**Reformed / Calvinist** - emphasizes God's sovereignty, predestination, total depravity, scripture as primary authority, expositional teaching. Authors: John Piper, Tim Keller, Voddie Baucham, R.C. Sproul.

**Charismatic / continuationist** - emphasizes the Holy Spirit's active gifts, healing, prophecy, deliverance, supernatural ministry. Authors: Bill Johnson, Heidi Baker, Randy Clark, Todd White.

**Wesleyan / Holiness** - emphasizes sanctification, holy living, free will, Wesleyan grace tradition. Authors: parts of the Nazarene, Wesleyan, and Salvation Army traditions.

**Mainline / progressive** - emphasizes social justice, inclusivity, liturgical formation, contemplative practices. Authors: Richard Rohr, parts of Rachel Held Evans's later work, Brian McLaren.

**Broadly evangelical / non-denominational** - avoids strong distinctives, emphasizes the gospel, personal relationship with Jesus, scripture as authoritative without strong second-order positions. Authors: Max Lucado, parts of Lysa TerKeurst, broadly seeker-friendly voices.

**Catholic** - emphasizes the sacraments, the saints, Mary, the magisterium, tradition alongside scripture. Authors: Scott Hahn, parts of Henri Nouwen, contemplative Catholic voices.

**Baptist / fundamentalist** - emphasizes scripture inerrancy, dispensational eschatology often, traditional gender roles, KJV preference common in older streams.

**Pentecostal / Word of Faith** - emphasizes the gifts of the Spirit, healing, prosperity in some streams, prophetic ministry, personal anointing.

**Step 3.2 - Stay Within the Author's Lane**

Once the lane is identified, Claude writes the page within that lane. This means:

**Use the lane's anchor scriptures.** A Reformed author's page should anchor in Romans, Ephesians, the doctrinal epistles. A charismatic author's page can anchor in Acts, 1 Corinthians 12, and the prophetic books. A contemplative author's page might anchor in the Psalms, Song of Songs, and contemplative passages.

**Use the lane's emphasis themes.** Reformed pages emphasize God's sovereignty over the reader's pain. Charismatic pages emphasize the Spirit's active work in the reader's situation. Wesleyan pages emphasize sanctification and growth in holiness. Match the theological emphasis to the lane.

**Avoid the lane's known objection points.** A charismatic author's page should not use language that triggers cessationist objections unnecessarily. A Reformed author's page should not use language that sounds open-theist or process-theological. A Catholic author's page should not use Protestant solas language ("scripture alone") if the author affirms tradition alongside scripture.

**Step 3.3 - Handle Cross-Lane Audiences Carefully**

Some offers target a deliberately broad audience that crosses theological lanes - _"for Christians of all traditions walking through suffering."_ When this is the intent (clarified in intake 3.5), Claude writes the page in the broadly evangelical / non-denominational lane by default, avoiding distinctive vocabulary from any specific tradition.

This means:

- No _breakthrough, anointing, prophetic_ (charismatic distinctives)
- No _doctrines of grace, sovereign election, expositional_ (Reformed distinctives)
- No _sanctification process, holiness tradition_ (Wesleyan distinctives)
- No _eucharist, magisterium, Marian_ (Catholic distinctives)

The page uses generic-to-evangelical vocabulary that any Christian tradition can read without theological friction. This narrows the page's voice but maximizes the audience.

**Step 3.4 - Theology Failure Modes**

**Lane mismatch.** Writing a charismatic page for a Reformed author, or vice versa. The author's audience reads the page and immediately senses the theology is off, even when they can't articulate why.

**Cross-lane drift.** Starting in one lane and drifting into another mid-page. A Reformed author's page that opens with sovereignty language but closes with breakthrough language reads as theologically confused.

**Over-claiming.** Making theological promises the offer cannot support - guaranteed healing, guaranteed breakthrough, guaranteed answered prayer. This crosses from voice calibration into prosperity-gospel territory regardless of the author's actual theology.

**Under-claiming.** Stripping the page of theological substance entirely to be "safe." The result is a page that doesn't sound like any specific Christian author - it sounds like Christian marketing, which converts worse than confident theological positioning within the author's actual lane.

**Dimension 4 - Denomination**

Denominational positioning is the cultural and tribal alignment of the page. Theology determines what's true; denomination determines which Christian tribe the page sounds like it belongs to.

**Step 4.1 - Identify the Author's Denominational Position**

From intake 3.5 and 5.4, Claude identifies where the author and the target audience sit denominationally. Common positions:

**Non-denominational evangelical** - modern worship music, casual dress culture, "doing life together" language, megachurch-adjacent vocabulary.

**Baptist** - Sunday school language, deacon and elder structure, hymn culture in older streams or worship band culture in younger, KJV or NKJV preference in older streams.

**Reformed Presbyterian** - confession-based, catechism-aware, expositional preaching culture, Bible study group language.

**Charismatic / Pentecostal** - worship night culture, prayer meeting language, prophetic ministry, deliverance, healing services.

**Mainline Protestant** - liturgical seasons, lectionary readings, denominational identity, social engagement vocabulary.

**Catholic** - mass, sacraments, saint-aware, parish language, devotional practices.

**Orthodox** - liturgy-centered, theosis language, icon-aware, ancient tradition emphasis.

**House church / organic church** - meeting-based language, no formal clergy vocabulary, mutual ministry emphasis.

**Step 4.2 - Match Denominational Vocabulary**

Each denomination has its own everyday vocabulary. The right page uses the right vocabulary without explanation, because the audience already speaks it.

**Non-denominational evangelical vocabulary:** _church family, life group, worship team, lead pastor, doing life together._

**Baptist vocabulary:** _Sunday school, prayer meeting, deacon, Wednesday night, revival, Sunday morning service._

**Reformed vocabulary:** _Bible study, expository preaching, the means of grace, confessional standards, family worship._

**Charismatic vocabulary:** _worship night, prayer room, prophetic ministry, breakthrough, anointing, fire, encounter._

**Mainline vocabulary:** _parish, liturgy, lectionary, advent, vestry, formation._

**Catholic vocabulary:** _mass, parish, sacraments, the Eucharist, Marian devotion, the saints, confession._

Using the audience's everyday vocabulary signals belonging. Using the wrong vocabulary signals outsider, even when the theology is correct.

**Step 4.3 - Handle Cross-Denominational Audiences**

When the offer targets multiple denominations (clarified in intake 3.5), Claude defaults to the broadly evangelical vocabulary with denominational-neutral phrasing. This means:

- _Church_ instead of _parish_ or _fellowship_
- _Pastor_ instead of _priest, minister, elder, bishop_
- _Bible study_ instead of _prayer meeting, life group, Sunday school, lectionary group_
- _Following Jesus_ instead of _discipleship, formation, sanctification, theosis_

The page sacrifices some denominational warmth for broader accessibility. This is the right trade-off when the audience is genuinely cross-denominational.

**Step 4.4 - Denomination Failure Modes**

**Using charismatic vocabulary on Reformed audiences.** _"Step into your breakthrough"_ on a Reformed page lands badly. _"Walk into the prophetic destiny God has for you"_ lands worse.

**Using Reformed vocabulary on charismatic audiences.** _"The doctrines of grace teach us..."_ on a charismatic page reads as cold and academic.

**Using Catholic vocabulary on Protestant audiences.** _"Through the sacraments..."_ on a Protestant page raises objections about works-righteousness.

**Using Protestant vocabulary on Catholic audiences.** _"Scripture alone teaches..."_ on a Catholic page raises objections about tradition.

**Mixed-denominational vocabulary salad.** Using _parish, life group, prophetic, expositional, and confessional_ all in one page. The audience can't tell who the page is for and assumes it's for nobody.

**The Four-Dimensional Calibration Workflow**

When Claude is calibrating voice for a specific author, the workflow is:

**Step 1 - Gather inputs.** Pull intake answers 3.5, 5.3, 5.4, 5.5, 5.6. Request a writing sample if not already provided.

**Step 2 - Identify the four dimensions.**

- Tone category (one of seven)
- Vocabulary register and signature words (catalog 5-10)
- Theological lane (one of eight)
- Denominational position (one of seven or eight, or broadly evangelical if cross-denominational)

**Step 3 - Write a calibration brief.** Before drafting the page, Claude writes a one-paragraph internal note summarizing the four dimensions. This becomes the reference Claude checks each section against.

Example calibration brief:

_"Author voice: pastoral / shepherding tone, mid-register vocabulary with signature words including 'beloved,' 'companion,' 'wilderness,' and 'doorway.' Theological lane: broadly evangelical with contemplative emphasis. Denomination: non-denominational, deliberately cross-denominational audience. Avoid: charismatic distinctives, Reformed distinctives, Catholic distinctives. Use: warm direct-address, image-rich metaphors, scripture from Psalms and the Gospels primarily."_

**Step 4 - Draft with the brief open.** Every section gets written with the calibration brief consulted. After each section, Claude re-reads and asks: _does this match the brief?_

**Step 5 - Audit at the end.** Once the draft is complete, Claude reads it as if encountering it for the first time and asks: _does this sound like the author or like generic Christian copy?_ Sections that fail get rewritten.

**What to Do When No Writing Sample Is Available**

Sometimes the user can't provide a writing sample - the author is new, hasn't published yet, or doesn't have an established voice. In this case:

**Ask the user to describe the author's voice in 3-5 adjectives.** _"Warm, direct, scripture-heavy, conversational, slightly humorous"_ gives Claude enough to make initial choices.

**Ask which existing Christian authors the author sounds like or wants to sound like.** _"Like Henri Nouwen meets Francis Chan"_ immediately calibrates tone and vocabulary in a useful way.

**Draft a first attempt and ask for feedback.** _"Here's a paragraph in the voice I'm hearing - does this sound like you?"_ The user's revision instincts will sharpen the calibration faster than any abstract description.

**Default to mid-register, warm, broadly evangelical** when truly no information is available. This is the safest default that converts adequately for most Christian audiences, though never as well as a fully calibrated voice would.

**The Underlying Principle**

Voice calibration is the difference between a page that the author would proudly publish and a page that the author would quietly edit before publishing. The technical structure can be perfect, the offer can be excellent, and the conversion psychology can be sound - but if the page doesn't sound like the author, the reader who knows the author senses the mismatch immediately, and the page underperforms.

Calibration is not about imitating the author's voice flawlessly - that's an impossible standard. It is about matching the author's voice closely enough that the page reads as authentic, so that everything else on the page (the offer, the value stack, the testimonials, the close) is received within a frame of trust the author has already built.

When in doubt about a voice choice, ask: _would the author wince at this sentence, or would they smile at it?_ Wince-triggering sentences get rewritten. Smile-triggering sentences stay. Over time, this single test produces pages that the author can sign their name to without revision - which is the standard voice calibration is trying to reach.
